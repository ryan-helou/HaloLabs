# QOVES / facial-analysis user research — what real customers say

Deep-research pass (2026-07-02) into **actual user sentiment** about QOVES and
adjacent facial-analysis products, to inform HaloLabs's onboarding and plan
format. This is the *demand-side* companion to
[qoves-teardown.md](qoves-teardown.md) (which covers their marketing site).

**Method & reliability.** Fan-out web search → 16 sources → 63 extracted claims
→ 25 put through a 3-vote adversarial verifier (need 2/3 to kill). 12 confirmed,
5 killed as misreadings, 8 left unverified when the run hit a rate limit — those
are quoted below but flagged. Reddit itself returned almost no indexed threads;
the real first-hand material came from **Trustpilot** (644 reviews, 4.8
headline that masks a consistent 1–2★ cluster), **looksmax.org** forum threads,
first-person **blog/Substack** reviews, a **leaked full 22-page QOVES PDF
report**, a **YouTube Umax teardown**, and a **Rolling Stone** BDD feature.
Treat forum/blog voices as vocal-minority, not representative — but they are
remarkably consistent on the format complaints, which is what we care about.

---

## 1. What users genuinely like

- **"Enhance, not change."** The single best-received framing. *"They did a
  great job at telling me what to enhance, not change… I recc everyone do this
  who needs a little ego boost."* Strengths-first reports reduce insecurity and
  boost confidence; one user said it made them appreciate features they used to
  dislike. (Trustpilot)
- **A prioritized roadmap that ends guessing.** *"It pointed out things I never
  noticed myself and gave me a clear idea of what to focus on instead of just
  guessing. I also liked that it highlighted my strengths and didn't just focus
  on things to improve… a good roadmap going forward."* (Trustpilot)
- **Per-feature breakdown with reasoning**, grounded in cited science — the
  measurement/analysis section is consistently called *"interesting"* and
  *"incredibly detailed"* even by people who hated the recommendations.
- **Psychological legitimacy.** The top-voted looksmax.org answer on "is it
  worth it" says the real value isn't better conclusions — it's permission:
  *"having someone with at least a veneer of professionalism give you some good
  starting points can make you feel more self-assured… yes it might be worth
  spending $150 just to not feel like plastic surgery research has to be a
  forbidden dark-net scary exploration."*
- **Fast delivery when it happens** — one May-2026 customer got the basic
  analysis in 2 days. (Speed is a *positive* differentiator precisely because
  the default is slow — see below.)

## 2. Complaints & pain points

- **★ THE central complaint: the advice is generic commodity filler that
  doesn't justify the price.** Verified, repeated verbatim across sources:
  - *"At the end of the report where they claim to give you advice it's just the
    usual crap: Vitamin C serum, SPF, lose bodyfat. So no, it's not helpful.
    Yes it is interesting but not 150$ worth of interesting."* (Trustpilot, Mar
    30 2026)
  - *"All they basically recommended me was: styling my hair, having a skincare
    routine (wtf, obviously) and growing my eyebrows (without telling me how to
    do that). And I remind you I PAYED 230 USD for that."* (Trustpilot, Apr 11
    2026)
- **The plan has no execution pathway.** Best-articulated gap, from a *positive*
  reviewer: *"I'd love a way of integrating my 'protocol'… how do I integrate
  all of these together so I actually know what to do and when?"* She called
  this the point where *"all health plans go to die."* (Alessa Weiler / Medium)
- **No specific products.** The report names *categories* ("brightening serum"),
  not products. One satisfied customer *still* had to paste the recommendations
  into ChatGPT to *"receive a super-comprehensive self-care plan with the names
  of specific skincare products."* The deliverable gap is doing the last mile
  yourself.
- **Recommendations skew surgical/injectable** (in the leaked report): the
  Closing Recommendations lead with septorhinoplasty, blepharoplasty, fillers,
  Botox, chin/cheek implants; all the achievable non-invasive advice is
  compressed into a couple of generic sentences.
- **Wait time + upsell layering.** ~28-day default turnaround, with an ~$80
  expedite upsell and a ~$25 haircut add-on. Slow *and* nickel-and-dimed.
- **Onboarding / account friction** (unverified — rate-limited — but direct
  quotes): account cancelled server-side forcing full repurchase and *"around 7
  hours re-uploading photos and repeating work"*; a refund *refused* inside the
  advertised 14-day window after *"additional unnecessary questions"*; a
  month-plus wait for a *"extremely disappointing"* report.
- **Accuracy, esp. color.** Trustpilot's own AI summary flags *"inaccurate
  analysis, particularly regarding color identification and the representation
  of their actual features… suggestions were generic or not tailored."*
- **They blame your photos.** QOVES's stock reply to accuracy complaints:
  *"the pictures you uploaded are very low quality and were taken with poor
  artificial lighting."* The report itself disclaims accuracy (can't control
  head position or lighting; would need radiographs). Photo-quality is the
  fault line of the whole category.

## 3. Report FORMAT — what gets praised vs. mocked

- **Mocked hardest: the before/after "projection" morph.** This is the most
  emotionally damaging element in the entire category.
  - QOVES: the AI-improved "after" clashed with the customer's identity — he
    *mocked* his better self: *"Looks sort of like a Miami club promoter, or at
    least a guy who really likes Dubai."* (verified)
  - Their public Adam Driver morph was ridiculed on looksmax.org for *"brow
    ridge reduction and nose reshaping [that] look like shit."*
  - Umax's "you as a 10/10": *"I changed race… this is a GTA-style version of
    what I would look like."* (verified)
  - Takeaway: a generic aesthetic imposed on your own face reads as *ridiculous
    or insulting*, not aspirational. **Projections backfire.**
- **No overall attractiveness score** in the QOVES comprehensive report — it's
  cephalometric measurements vs. published averages (Farkas, Powell, Wen), not
  a 1–10. Users who *wanted* a score went to Umax; users who feared one
  appreciated its absence.
- **Format critique that recurs:** scoring against *universal* "ideal metrics"
  instead of maximizing the individual's own archetype. *"Instead of trying to
  attain an ideal you should try to maximize what you have. QOVES by nature
  focuses on the former."* Users want risk/reward-weighted, archetype-aware
  advice: *"some tradeoffs are simply not worth it. Analyzing what's worth
  changing based on your ratios is key."*

## 4. Competitors — Umax / LooksMax AI (the app tier)

- **Hard paywall on your own results:** upload selfies, then results are locked
  behind $4/week auto-renew (no free trial) *or* invite 3 friends. The single
  most-cited Play Store complaint.
- **Non-deterministic scoring destroys trust:** *"I submitted the same picture 3
  times and almost every time a different number."* Same face, different score →
  users conclude the whole thing is fake.
- **Generic boilerplate:** *"cleanse twice a day, chew gum, talk to a
  dermatologist… don't get an app that tells you to talk to a dermatologist."*
- **Category-wide skepticism:** on the core looksmaxxing forum the flat
  consensus is *"any rate app is scam."* The bar for a *credible* analysis
  product is low but the trust deficit is high.

## 5. Psychological / ethical reactions (the guardrail evidence)

From Rolling Stone's reporting on clinical psychologist **Dr. Toni Pikoos**, a
BDD specialist (all 3-0 verified):

- Clients using AI to rate their attractiveness is now *"almost coming up in
  every single session"* — uploading photos, asking for 10-point ratings.
- **Worst case is real:** *"The worst-case scenario is, their symptoms will get
  worse"* — up to suicidal ideation, plus dependency on reassurance-seeking and
  reinforced distorted self-perception.
- **AI feedback is uniquely dangerous because it reads as objective:** chatbots
  appear *"so authoritative"* that users treat outputs as *"factual and
  impartial,"* which makes a negative rating *"harder to challenge"* than human
  reassurance.

This is the strongest external validation of HaloLabs's no-scores / strengths-
first / wellbeing-note posture. It is not just nicer — it is the difference
between a safe product and a harmful one, from a clinician on the record.

## 6. What users say they actually WANTED (the ideal deliverable)

Synthesized across all sources — the deliverable people describe wanting:

1. **An execution pathway, not a diagnosis.** "What do I do, and when" — daily/
   weekly integration, sequencing. This is the #1 unmet need.
2. **Prioritized by risk/reward and attainability**, not by measurement gap.
   Cheap/reversible/high-impact first; keep features that serve your harmony.
3. **Specific named products** (with prices), so nobody has to re-derive the
   plan in ChatGPT.
4. **Strengths named explicitly**, first, as their own section — the "ego boost"
   is a feature, not padding.
5. **Archetype-aware:** maximize the person you are, don't push you toward a
   generic "prettyboy" ideal.
6. **Fast, and honest about photo quality up front** so the analysis is
   trustworthy and nobody feels blamed later.

## 7. Implications for HaloLabs (prioritized)

The headline: **HaloLabs's existing strategy is already aimed at exactly the
gaps competitors leave.** Every top complaint (generic advice, no execution
path, no products, mocked morphs, scores that hurt) is something STRATEGY.md
already says to do the opposite of. The work is to *sharpen and prove* it, not
pivot. Concrete, ranked:

### Plan format
1. **Make the execution pathway the spine of the plan, not an afterthought.**
   The routine (AM/PM/weekly) and the 3-phase roadmap are our biggest
   competitive moat — QOVES's own happy customers begged for exactly this.
   Lead the plan with "here's your week," not "here are 17 observations."
2. **Every suggestion keeps its `why` (personal) + `how[]` (steps) +
   `products[]` (named, priced) + `frequency`/`timeline`.** This is already in
   schema v2 — the research says it's the whole ballgame. Never ship a
   suggestion that's just a noun ("grow your eyebrows") without the how.
3. **Strengths as a first-class, first-placed section.** "What's already working
   for you" before anything to improve. The most-loved QOVES trait, and a BDD
   safeguard.
4. **Rank by effort/impact (quick wins), never by measurement deficit.** Keep
   the "quick win" tags. Frame around maximizing the user's archetype; the plan
   summary should say what to *enhance*, echoing "enhance, not change."
5. **Hold the line on no before/after morphs of the user's own face.** The
   research is unambiguous: projections are the most mocked, most identity-
   clashing, most harmful element across every product. If we ever do
   before/after, it's *real* progress photos the user took — never a generated
   "improved you." (STRATEGY §3.4 guardrail confirmed by evidence.)
6. **No overall attractiveness score — stay the course.** Clinician-backed:
   authoritative-seeming numeric ratings are the specific mechanism that worsens
   BDD. Our tags-describe-suggestions rule is the right one.

### Onboarding
7. **Nail photo quality *at capture*, and never blame the user later.** QOVES's
   whole trust problem is bad photos → bad analysis → "your fault." Our guided
   capture flow (framing, lighting tips, face-guide ellipse) is the right
   instinct — make it impossible to submit unusable photos rather than
   apologizing for them afterward. Consider a lightweight quality check before
   "Begin analysis."
8. **Local-first + instant is a real wedge.** 28-day waits, expedite upsells,
   paywalled results, and account-cancellation data loss are *the* competitor
   pain points. "Your photos never leave your machine, and you get your plan in
   minutes, free" attacks all of them at once. Say it loud in onboarding.
9. **Ask enough to personalize, so the plan can't read as boilerplate.** The
   "not tailored to me" complaint is fatal. Our onboarding (goals, time, budget,
   no-gos, what they've tried) exists to make every suggestion cite something
   the user told us — the `builtFor` echo chips and personal `why` are the
   antidote. Make the plan visibly reference their onboarding answers.
10. **Frame the intake around wellbeing, not aspiration-max.** Keep the 18+ gate
    and the "aiming for a better version of *yourself*" framing. It's both the
    ethical floor and, per the "ego boost / enhance not change" reviews, what
    the best-received experiences feel like.
